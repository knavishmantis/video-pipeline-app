# Kallaway Short-Form Video Frameworks

The following frameworks are from Kane Kallaway (1B+ views, @kallawaymarketing), extracted from 10 of his educational videos on hooks, scripting, storytelling, and common mistakes. Apply these when writing scripts for the KnavishMantis channel.

Source videos: "How to write a script so good viewers can't stop watching", "How to Script Viral Videos 10x Faster", "How to Create a Killer Hook", "Say This in Your Videos (Story Locks)", "How to Become a Storytelling Genius (Dopamine Ladders)", "Storytelling Is Easier Than You Think (Story Loops)", "The ONLY 6 Words You Need to Hook ANY Viewer", "Copy This Hook (Desire-Based Hooks)", "Give me 18 minutes (4 Buckets of Mistakes)", "I Studied 10,000+ Videos (7 Deadly Pitfalls)".

## The 3-Step Hook Formula

1. **Context Lean-In**: Clearly establish what the video is about while providing a reason to care. Use a surprising detail.
2. **Pattern Interrupt / Scroll Stop**: Use a contrast word ("But," "However") to create a cognitive gap.
3. **Contrarian Snapback**: Flip the narrative with an unexpected angle that contradicts initial expectations.

## The 6 Hook Power Words

Every hook is built from these components stacked together:
1. **Subject Clarity** — Who (I, you, we, or a proper noun)
2. **Action Word** — The verb (grew, built, found, broke)
3. **Objective/End-State** — The result, as shocking as possible
4. **Contrast Word** — Compares base state to new reality ("but" is the most powerful)
5. **Proof Word** (optional) — Adds credibility (e.g., "again" implies you've done it before)
6. **Time Word** (optional) — Adds urgency ("in 5 months", "in 90 days")

## 9 Proven Hook Formats

1. **Secret Reveal** — Teases an unknown insight ("Google just released...")
2. **Case Study** — Highlights someone who achieved a result unexpectedly
3. **Comparison** — Compares two versions of something
4. **Question** — Asks a question to plant curiosity directly
5. **Education** — Introduces a step-by-step process
6. **List** — Introduces an ordered set of items
7. **Contrarian** — Bold against-the-grain take first sentence
8. **Personal Experience** — Introduces a personal story
9. **Problem** — Introduces a specific pain point

**KnavishMantis primarily uses: Question, Contrarian, Secret Reveal, and Problem hooks.**

## 4 Hook Commandments (Post-Writing Checklist)

1. **Alignment** — Visual, spoken, and text hook must mean the same thing
2. **Speed to Value** — Zero delay, zero fluff
3. **Clarity** — Unmistakable what the video is about
4. **Curiosity** — Opens a question in the viewer's mind

## Script Structure: The Setup/Rehook Dance

Kallaway rejects simple hook-body-conclusion. Instead:

**Hook → Setup/Rehook → Setup/Rehook → Setup/Rehook → Outro**

Each cycle: provide context → use a contrast word to tease that something better/crazier is coming → deliver the reveal → open a new loop.

For shorts (~60-90 seconds): use ~2 rehooks.

**Rehook phrases (loop openers):**
- "Here's the thing, though."
- "But that's not even the most interesting part."
- "And this is where it gets crazy."
- "Most people stop here. But actually..."
- "Here's what nobody talks about."

**KnavishMantis already does this naturally** — e.g., "You might think X but actually Y" and "But here's the thing nobody talks about" appear throughout existing scripts.

## The "But / Therefore" Rule (Replace "And Then")

Popularized by South Park creators Trey Parker and Matt Stone, adopted by Kallaway. The core principle: **every beat in your script should be connected by "but" or "therefore", never "and then."**

- **"And then"** = things happen independently, no tension, no causality. The viewer checks out.
- **"But"** = an obstacle or contradiction. Creates conflict and opens a curiosity loop.
- **"Therefore"** = a consequence. Creates causality and forward momentum. Note: "therefore" is the concept — in practice, use casual equivalents like **"so"**, **"meaning"**, **"which means"** for Minecraft content. "Therefore" sounds too formal/posh for this audience.

**Bad (and then):** "Dogs only have 10 hearts, and then a creeper does 32 damage, and then your dog dies."

**Good (but/therefore):** "Dogs only have 10 hearts. But a single creeper does 32 damage — therefore your dog gets instantly vaporized even if you survive."

The "but" creates the conflict (the damage is way more than their health). The "therefore" delivers the consequence (they die, you don't). The viewer feels the logic chain pulling them forward.

**Audit your scripts:** Read each sentence transition. If two beats are connected by "and" or "and then," ask: is there a causal or conflicting relationship? If yes, replace with "but" or "therefore." If no relationship exists, one of the beats is probably unnecessary — cut it.

Kallaway uses "but" ~26 times per video on average. It is the single most important word in short-form scripting.

## 7 Story Structures

1. **Breakdown** — Explain a concept in building blocks (Hook → Context/shock → How it works → Why it matters)
2. **Newscaster** — Factual recounting of an event
3. **Case Study Explainer** — Educate on a blueprint/framework
4. **Listicle** — Ordered list of items
5. **Problem Solver** — Agitate a pain, tee up a solution
6. **Tutorial** — Step-by-step walkthrough
7. **Personal Story** — First-person storytelling

**KnavishMantis primarily uses: Breakdown (#1), Contrarian/Problem Solver (#5), and occasional Listicle (#4).**

## The 6 Story Locks (Storytelling Techniques)

1. **Term Branding** — Give your concepts a NAME. The moment something has a name, it feels more important. (KM example: "the 90-20 rule" in the productivity script)
2. **Embedded Truths** — Frame statements as already true. Replace "if you try this" → "when you try this", "this might work" → "the reason this works so well". Remove: if, maybe, might, could, probably.
3. **Thought Narration** — Say what the viewer is thinking: "Now you're probably wondering...", "You might be saying..."  (KM already does this heavily)
4. **Negative Frames** — Frame advice as warnings. "This is killing your X" > "Here's how to improve X". Loss aversion is 2x stronger than gain motivation.
5. **Loop Openers** — Transitional phrases with contrast words that reset the attention clock (see rehook phrases above)
6. **Contrast Words** — The engine of all storytelling. "But", "actually", "instead", "turns out", "except", "yet". Build contrast into every major sentence.

## The Dopamine Ladder (6 Levels)

How viewer attention escalates through a video:

1. **Stimulation** (first 1-2 sec) — Visual stun: colors, motion, contrast
2. **Captivation** — Curiosity from an open question
3. **Anticipation** — Viewer starts guessing, you yank the answer away with a misdirection, then reset
4. **Validation** — Close the loop. Reward with something non-obvious.
5. **Affection** — Viewer likes the creator/personality
6. **Revelation** — Pavlovian: viewer gets dopamine just seeing your name

## Curiosity Loops

Every story is a series of loops: **Context (first half) → Reveal (second half)**. As soon as one loop closes, another opens.

**Building loops:**
1. List all facts/takes about the topic
2. Score each on "shock score" (1-100: how many out of 100 people would be shocked?)
3. Highest-scoring facts become REVEALS (second half of loops)
4. Work BACKWARDS: figure out what context is needed so the reveal makes sense
5. Sequence: broadest context first, then build each loop on previous context

For shorts: 3-4 loops total.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Scripts:**
- **Over-stuffing**: One topic, one takeaway, get out. Don't cram 3-5 ideas.
- **Poor speed to value**: Value is buried too late. Bold where value starts, then move it earlier.
- **Fluff sentences**: Apply the "$100-per-word" exercise — imagine paying $100 per word.
- **Passive voice / complex vocabulary**: Target 6th grade reading level, active voice.

**Hooks:**
- Three-hook misalignment (visual, spoken, text say different things)
- Not curious enough — introduce new topics RELATIVE to something they already know
- Viewer doesn't understand why the video matters to them personally

**Pacing:**
- Use short, punchy sentences. Mix lengths for rhythm.
- Use the "closed eye test": listen audio-only — if it bores you, cut space between sentences
- Restate complex things twice, second time simpler: "rudimentary — or simple"

## Applying Kallaway to KnavishMantis Scripts

When writing a KnavishMantis script, layer these frameworks onto the existing channel voice:

1. **Hook**: Use the 3-step formula (Context Lean → Pattern Interrupt → Contrarian Snapback). KM already does this — formalize it.
2. **Score your angle**: Before writing, list 5-10 possible angles, shock-score each, pick the highest.
3. **Pick a story structure**: Usually Breakdown or Problem Solver for this channel.
4. **Write in loops**: Each paragraph should be a context → reveal cycle. Close one loop, open the next.
5. **Add 2 rehooks**: At ~20 seconds and ~40 seconds, drop a loop opener ("But here's the thing...", "And this is where it gets crazy").
6. **Apply story locks**: Use embedded truths (remove "maybe/might"), thought narration ("You're probably thinking..."), and contrast words ("but", "actually") in every major sentence.
7. **End with a dopamine payoff**: The final reveal should be the most shocking/funny fact — save your best for last.
